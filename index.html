    01 Profile vault

    Set the Correct Patient First

    Open the right patient record before any agent starts reasoning.

    • Use forOpening a new patient or refreshing an existing profile before any agent run.
    • Tell Care NovaAge, conditions, medicines, allergies, baseline vitals, care team, language, and emergency contact.
    • You getOne clean patient memory that every tab can reuse without mixing people.
    • Next moveOnly open an agent after the active patient and baseline facts look correct.
    Precision check: the active patient name and health context should match before pressing Run. Example: "Naveed, 52, hypertension, type 2 diabetes, metformin, baseline BP 130/85, no allergies."
    02 General agent

    Use General for the First Safe Answer

    Start here when the safest first pass matters more than deep specialization.

    • Use forPlain health questions, mild symptoms, prevention questions, or cases where the right tab is not obvious.
    • Tell Care NovaThe main concern, duration, severity, readings, medicines, recent changes, and warning signs.
    • You getOne patient-friendly next step shaped by routing, local memory, and safety boundaries.
    • Next moveSwitch to Specialist, Vitals, Medicine, Labs, Visits, or Safety when the case becomes focused.
    Precision check: one concern, one purpose, one next step. Do not mix many unrelated questions. Example: "Headache since morning, 4/10, BP 130/85, no fever or chest pain. What should I watch for?"
    03 Specialist agent

    Use Specialist for Core Disease Review

    Move here when you need disease-specific reasoning instead of a broad first answer.

    • Use forDiabetes, BP, cardiac, respiratory, kidney, thyroid, infection, pain, and chronic-disease review.
    • Tell Care NovaThe diagnosis if known, medicines, recent symptoms, readings, labs, and what changed from baseline.
    • You getA focused breakdown of patterns, risk cues, monitoring needs, and clinician questions.
    • Next moveGenerate Summary after the review when you need a clinician-ready disease handoff.
    Precision check: use Specialist only for disease-focused review, not claims, booking, or record storage. Example: "Review diabetes control: HbA1c 8.2, fasting sugar 160, metformin, tired after meals."
    04 Medical library

    Use Atlas Before Complex Questions

    Learn the topic first, then ask a patient-specific question with better context.

    • Use forDiseases, symptoms, medicines, tests, prevention topics, and warning-sign learning.
    • Tell Care NovaThe condition, body system, medicine, test, or precaution you want explained.
    • You getBackground, common patterns, precautions, and questions worth asking later.
    • Next moveReturn to General or Specialist when you need patient-specific guidance.
    Precision check: Atlas explains topics; it should not replace clinician diagnosis or treatment decisions. Example: "Open an asthma guide with triggers, warning signs, inhaler questions, and prevention tips."
    05 Vitals specialist

    Review Readings With Context

    A reading becomes useful when timing, symptoms, and repeats turn it into a pattern.

    • Use forBP, sugar, pulse, oxygen, temperature, weight, BMI, and trend review.
    • Tell Care NovaThe value, timing, repeat status, symptoms, food or exercise context, and medicine timing.
    • You getPattern review, urgency clues, and what is worth monitoring or discussing.
    • Next moveSave repeated readings so Summary can show a trend instead of a single number.
    Precision check: a reading becomes useful only when time, symptom, and repeat context are included. Example: "BP 160/98 twice today, headache 5/10, missed BP medicine yesterday, no chest pain."
    06 Medicine guide

    Ask Medicine Questions Safely

    Keep medicine support inside timing, safety, and interaction boundaries.

    • Use forPurpose, missed dose, timing, side effects, and interaction questions.
    • Tell Care NovaMedicine name, strength if known, schedule, last dose, side effects, and other medicines or supplements.
    • You getSafe guidance on timing, common issues, red flags, and who to ask next.
    • Next moveKeep dose changes, prescribing, and dose calculations with a clinician or pharmacist.
    Precision check: include the prescription context; never ask the app to decide a dose change. Example: "Metformin 500 mg, missed morning dose, mild stomach upset, also taking amlodipine."
    07 Lab report reader

    Turn Lab Reports Into Clear Questions

    Ranges and dates matter as much as the value itself.

    • Use forReport values that need plain-language explanation and follow-up direction.
    • Tell Care NovaTest name, value, unit, reference range, report date, previous result, symptoms, and medicines.
    • You getWhat the result may mean, whether it looks like a trend, and what to ask next.
    • Next moveSave confirmed findings to Records so later summaries can use the lab history.
    Precision check: value + unit + range + date gives a better answer than value alone. Example: "HbA1c 8.2%, previous 7.6%, LDL 160 mg/dL, report date June 25, tired after meals."
    08 Wellness coach

    Build Daily Health Habits

    Use Wellness for routines and prevention, not for worsening risk.

    • Use forDiet, sleep, walking, hydration, stress, and routine-building.
    • Tell Care NovaAge, conditions, current habits, activity level, and one goal to improve.
    • You getA realistic routine with checkpoints, tracking ideas, and prevention tips.
    • Next moveSwitch to Safety or Specialist if symptoms or disease worsening enter the picture.
    Precision check: choose one habit goal per run, then track it for at least a week. Example: "Age 52, diabetes, sedentary work, poor sleep, want a safe walking and hydration plan."
    09 Visit agent

    Prepare Hospital-Ready Appointments

    A strong visit brief is short enough for staff and useful enough for triage.

    • Use forBooking help, follow-up planning, and doctor-question preparation.
    • Tell Care NovaThe reason for visit, symptoms, readings, reports, medicines, preferred date, and care goal.
    • You getA short appointment brief, checklist, and follow-up questions.
    • Next moveAfter the visit, store instructions and pending tests in Records.
    Precision check: appointment notes should be short enough for hospital staff but complete enough for triage. Example: "Plan cardiology follow-up for repeated BP 160/98, fatigue, medicine review, and ECG question."
    10 Safety center

    Escalate Warning Signs First

    Urgency should outrank convenience the moment warning signs appear.

    • Use forChest pain, breathing trouble, fainting, weakness, severe allergy, bleeding, or rapid worsening.
    • Tell Care NovaThe exact symptom, start time, severity, vitals, medicines, conditions, and whether someone is present.
    • You getThe risk level, immediate next action, and the key information to share.
    • Next moveRecord the event after urgent action so later review has the right timeline.
    Precision check: warning signs override normal workflows. Safety comes before convenience. Example: "Chest pressure 20 minutes, sweating, shortness of breath, diabetes, pulse 120, alone at home."
    11 Records vault

    Save Only Useful Patient Evidence

    The next answer improves only when the saved facts are clean and durable.

    • Use forSaving the patient facts that should matter later across agents and summaries.
    • Tell Care NovaThe record type, date, short title, source tab, and the one fact that must persist.
    • You getCleaner memory, less duplicate clutter, and stronger later summaries.
    • Next moveDelete vague or outdated drafts so future answers stay sharp.
    Precision check: save verified facts and final outputs, not every rough draft. Example: "Save lab summary: HbA1c 8.2 on June 25, discuss diabetes control at next visit."
    12 Insurance navigator

    Organize Claims and Coverage

    Insurance runs faster when claim facts stay separate from medical reasoning.

    • Use forClaims, coverage questions, EOB issues, pre-authorization, and appeal preparation.
    • Tell Care NovaThe insurer, claim type, service date, provider, billed amount, documents, and the issue.
    • You getA missing-document list, policy questions, and next-contact checklist.
    • Next moveKeep billing facts and medical facts separate for cleaner claim handling.
    Precision check: insurance output prepares a claim; it does not guarantee approval. Example: "Prepare claim checklist for hospital bill, doctor note, lab report, prescription, and policy card."
    13 Summary report

    Create a Doctor-Ready Handoff

    Summary is where the best facts turn into one share-ready patient story.

    • Use forDoctor visits, family updates, and download-ready patient handoffs.
    • Tell Care NovaWhich saved facts, symptoms, vitals, labs, medicine issues, and open questions matter now.
    • You getOne structured summary with risk, timeline, next actions, and discussion points.
    • Next moveShare only the minimum needed and keep the rest local.
    Precision check: Summary is strongest after Records has useful patient-specific facts. Example: "Build a doctor note for high BP trend, missed medicine, headache, and next visit questions."
    14 Online and offline

    Use Local-First Continuity

    The workflow should stay useful even when the network or local server drops.

    • Use forPrivate review, low-connectivity use, and continuity when the server or network is unavailable.
    • Tell Care NovaThe same patient context and question you would use online.
    • You getLocal memory, curated knowledge, and safe guidance even without cloud enrichment.
    • Next moveGo online only when you need refreshed references or configured external services.
    Precision check: local records are the continuity layer; online access is optional enrichment. Example: "Use offline for private review; use online when you need refreshed reference context."
    15 Modes and language

    Choose Guided or Expert Mode

    Mode is about who is using the app, not about which condition is being reviewed.

    • Use forMatching the interface to the user's comfort level and workflow depth.
    • Tell Care NovaNothing new; switch Guided or Expert before or during the same patient run.
    • You getSimpler steps in Guided and deeper controls with denser detail in Expert.
    • Next moveTreat theme and language as readability settings, not logic changes.
    Precision check: switch mode based on the user, not the medical concern. Example: "Use Guided for simple care conversations; use Expert when reviewing detailed records or reports."
    16 Quality loop

    Improve Accuracy Every Run

    Better inputs and cleaner memory are the two fastest ways to improve output quality.

    • Use forImproving answer quality across repeated runs and keeping the workflow disciplined.
    • Tell Care NovaOne focused question after the profile, records, and latest facts are current.
    • You getTighter routing, better evidence fit, and cleaner next actions.
    • Next moveRepeat the loop after every new symptom, report, visit, or medicine change.
    Precision check: better inputs plus cleaner records produce more useful future answers. Example: "Use the same loop each time: Profile -> Ask -> Route -> Review -> Save -> Summary."

                                                                                                                                                                        Safety Decision Levels

                                                                                                                                                                        Emergency now Severe or sudden warning signs: breathing trouble, chest pressure, stroke signs, fainting, seizure, blue lips, major bleeding, or unsafe behavior.
                                                                                                                                                                        Urgent today Symptoms are worsening, unusual for the patient, linked with high-risk readings, or need same-day clinician guidance.
                                                                                                                                                                        Clinician follow-up Symptoms continue, medicine side effects are possible, readings are repeatedly outside usual range, or reports need review.
                                                                                                                                                                        Safe monitoring Stable concern with no warning signs: track changes, follow existing care plan, and prepare questions for the next visit.

                                                                                                                                                                        Urgent Warning Signs

                                                                                                                                                                        Use urgent real-world care when symptoms suggest immediate risk. Do not wait for an app response if the person looks seriously unwell.

                                                                                                                                                                        Chest or breathing Chest pain or pressure, arm or jaw pain, sweating, trouble breathing, blue lips, or fainting.
                                                                                                                                                                        Stroke or brain Face droop, one-sided weakness, slurred speech, sudden confusion, sudden severe headache, vision or balance trouble.
                                                                                                                                                                        Allergy or airway Swollen lips, tongue, face, or throat; wheezing; severe rash with dizziness; or breathing difficulty.
                                                                                                                                                                        Bleeding, injury, poison Heavy bleeding, serious burn, deep wound, possible broken bone, head injury with vomiting or confusion, poison or overdose concern.
                                                                                                                                                                        Fever or dehydration Fever with stiff neck, confusion, severe weakness, repeated vomiting, very low urine, extreme sleepiness, or rapid worsening.
                                                                                                                                                                        Mental safety Self-harm risk, unsafe behavior, severe agitation, confusion, intoxication with danger, or risk of harming others.
                                                                                                                                                                        Call local emergency services or go to urgent care if these signs are active.

                                                                                                                                                                        Safety Topic Library

                                                                                                                                                                        Heart & BP Chest pressure, fainting, severe breathlessness, stroke signs, or very high BP with symptoms needs urgent care.
                                                                                                                                                                        Diabetes & sugar Confusion, fainting, shaking, sweating, vomiting, extreme thirst, or very low/high sugar with symptoms needs prompt help.
                                                                                                                                                                        Breathing Breathing effort, blue lips, severe wheeze, inability to speak normally, or oxygen drop should not wait.
                                                                                                                                                                        Medicine safety New swelling, rash with dizziness, bleeding, severe stomach pain, overdose concern, or duplicate medicines need review.
                                                                                                                                                                        Children, pregnancy, seniors Use extra caution for infants, pregnancy, older adults, weak immunity, falls, dehydration, fever, or confusion.
                                                                                                                                                                        Post-surgery or discharge Worsening pain, fever, wound drainage, swelling, new weakness, breathing trouble, or missed follow-up needs attention.

                                                                                                                                                                        Stroke F.A.S.T. Check

                                                                                                                                                                        • Face: ask the person to smile; check if one side droops.
                                                                                                                                                                        • Arms: ask them to raise both arms; check if one arm drifts downward.
                                                                                                                                                                        • Speech: ask for a simple sentence; listen for slurred or strange speech.
                                                                                                                                                                        • Time: if any sign is present, use emergency care immediately.

                                                                                                                                                                        Immediate Action Plan First Safe Actions

                                                                                                                                                                          Before You Call Care

                                                                                                                                                                          • Say the main problem first: symptom, severity, start time, and whether it is getting worse.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Share age, major conditions, allergies, pregnancy status if relevant, and immune-risk status.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Read current vitals aloud: BP, pulse, oxygen, sugar, temperature, pain level, and repeat readings.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Give medicine names, last dose time, missed doses, new medicines, supplements, alcohol, or overdose concern.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Explain support: alone or with someone, safe transport, caregiver contact, and preferred hospital or clinic.

                                                                                                                                                                          Medicine Precautions

                                                                                                                                                                          • Keep an updated medicine list with names, timing, allergies, and the prescribing clinician.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Do not double doses after a missed medicine unless the prescription label or clinician says so.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Ask a pharmacist before combining new over-the-counter medicines, supplements, or pain relievers.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Report new swelling, dizziness, rash, breathing changes, unusual bleeding, or severe stomach symptoms.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Check expiration dates, labels, duplicate ingredients, alcohol warnings, driving warnings, pregnancy warnings, and storage instructions.
                                                                                                                                                                          Allergies Missed dose Side effects Interactions Expiration Storage

                                                                                                                                                                          Vitals Monitoring

                                                                                                                                                                          • Track blood pressure, pulse, temperature, and sugar with date, time, symptoms, meal timing, and medicine timing.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Repeat an unusual reading after resting when safe, then compare with your usual pattern.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Very high or very low readings with chest pain, fainting, confusion, weakness, or breathing trouble need urgent care.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Bring trend logs to visits so clinicians can review patterns instead of one isolated number.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Record device type, cuff placement, posture, recent caffeine/activity, and whether the reading was repeated correctly.

                                                                                                                                                                          Fever, Infection & Dehydration

                                                                                                                                                                          • Track temperature, fluids, urination, breathing, alertness, vomiting, diarrhea, and how symptoms change.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Use hand hygiene, masks when appropriate, clean shared surfaces, and avoid close contact when contagious symptoms are present.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Seek real care sooner for infants, pregnancy, older adults, weak immunity, severe pain, stiff neck, rash, confusion, or breathing trouble.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Do not delay care if dehydration, severe weakness, persistent vomiting, or rapidly worsening fever appears.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Keep a hydration log and note dizziness, dry mouth, reduced urination, inability to keep fluids down, or worsening alertness.

                                                                                                                                                                          Do / Avoid

                                                                                                                                                                          Do
                                                                                                                                                                          • Use emergency care for severe, sudden, or rapidly worsening symptoms.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Keep the person seated or lying safely if dizzy, faint, weak, or confused.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Bring medicines, allergies, reports, readings, discharge papers, and emergency contacts.
                                                                                                                                                                          Avoid
                                                                                                                                                                          • Do not drive yourself if chest pain, fainting, confusion, severe weakness, or breathing trouble is active.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Do not change doses, mix medicines, or stop essential medicines without clinician guidance.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Do not wait for the app if warning signs are active or the person looks seriously unwell.

                                                                                                                                                                          Home Safety Measures

                                                                                                                                                                          • Stay hydrated as advised, eat regular meals when appropriate, and avoid sudden heavy activity during illness.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Keep emergency contacts, allergies, current medicines, and major conditions easy to access.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Use hand hygiene, clean shared surfaces, and avoid close contact when fever, cough, vomiting, or diarrhea is present.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Reduce fall risk by keeping walkways clear, using good lighting, and sitting down if dizzy or weak.

                                                                                                                                                                          Falls, Seniors & Daily Setup

                                                                                                                                                                          • Use clear walkways, non-slip footwear, night lights, reachable water, and support when standing after illness.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Review dizziness, sleep medicines, low sugar symptoms, dehydration, vision changes, or recent falls with a clinician.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Keep a simple emergency card: name, age, conditions, medicines, allergies, doctor, caregiver, and preferred hospital.
                                                                                                                                                                          • For people living alone, arrange check-in calls during fever, dizziness, new weakness, or medication changes.
                                                                                                                                                                          • After a fall, check breathing, pulse, bleeding, head injury signs, pain, movement limits, and whether the person can safely stand.

                                                                                                                                                                          Caregiver Safety Setup

                                                                                                                                                                          • Keep a simple card with patient name, age, conditions, medicines, allergies, doctor, caregiver, and hospital preference.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Set check-in times during fever, dizziness, medication changes, new weakness, or recovery after discharge.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Keep phone charged, transport ready, key documents visible, and emergency numbers easy to access.
                                                                                                                                                                          • For confusion, falls, or faintness, do not leave the person alone until safety is clear.

                                                                                                                                                                          Mental Health Crisis

                                                                                                                                                                          • If there is risk of self-harm, harming others, severe confusion, or unsafe behavior, get immediate real-world support.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Stay with the person if safe, remove obvious hazards, and contact emergency services, crisis support, or a trusted caregiver.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Do not leave a person alone if they are actively suicidal, extremely agitated, intoxicated, or unable to stay safe.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Use calm, short words: listen, reduce stimulation, and focus on getting help rather than debating.
                                                                                                                                                                          • If immediate danger is possible, prioritize emergency help over collecting more details.

                                                                                                                                                                          Care Visit Preparation

                                                                                                                                                                          • Prepare a short timeline: when symptoms started, what changed, what helps, and what makes it worse.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Take medicine bottles, recent reports, home readings, discharge papers, and questions for the clinician.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Ask what warning signs should trigger urgent care, what to monitor at home, and when to follow up.
                                                                                                                                                                          • Confirm the care plan in simple words before leaving the visit.
